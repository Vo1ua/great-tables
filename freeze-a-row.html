How to Freeze a Row in Google Sheets (3 Easy Methods)

Scroll down a long spreadsheet and your header row disappears, leaving you guessing which column is which. Freezing a row fixes this — it pins one or more rows in place at the top of the sheet, so they stay visible no matter how far down you scroll. Here are three ways to do it, depending on what's fastest for you.

Method 1: Using the View Menu (Most Reliable)

  1. Click any cell in the row you want to freeze — for example, click a cell in row 1.
  2. Click View in the top menu.
  3. Hover over Freeze.
  4. Select 1 row to freeze just the top row, or choose Up to current row to freeze everything from the top down to the row you clicked.
This method works no matter where your cursor was before, and it's the easiest one to repeat consistently — which is why it's worth learning first.

Method 2: Drag the Freeze Line

Look at the top-left corner of your sheet, where the row numbers and column letters meet. You'll notice a thicker gray line running along the bottom edge of that corner box.

  1. Hover over that thick gray line until your cursor turns into a hand icon.
  2. Click and drag the line downward, past the row(s) you want to freeze.
  3. Release the mouse — everything above the line is now frozen.

The same trick works for columns: drag the vertical gray line (at the same corner) to the right instead of down.

Freezing Multiple Rows

Both methods above support freezing more than one row. With the View menu, select a cell in the last row you want frozen, then choose Up to current row. With the drag method, just drag the gray line further down, past all the rows you want included.

How to Unfreeze a Row

  1. Click View in the top menu.
  2. Hover over Freeze.
  3. Select No rows.

Alternatively, drag the gray freeze line back up to the very top of the sheet.

Freezing a Row on Mobile

The Google Sheets mobile app uses a slightly different path since there's no visible menu bar:

  1. Open your spreadsheet in the Google Sheets app.
  2. Tap the sheet name/tab at the top to open its options.
  3. Scroll to Freeze rows.
  4. Use the up and down arrows to set how many rows to freeze.

Common Issues

I Can Only Freeze Rows From the Top

This is expected — Google Sheets only supports freezing rows starting from row 1 downward, not rows in the middle of your data. If you need a specific row pinned, you'll need to move it to the top first (for example by sorting).

Frozen Rows Look Misaligned After Editing

Adding or removing columns can sometimes shift how frozen rows line up visually. Unfreezing and re-freezing usually resolves this.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does freezing a row also freeze it when printing?

Not automatically — check your print settings for an option to repeat frozen rows on every printed page, since freezing on-screen and freezing for print are handled separately.

Can I freeze rows and columns at the same time?

Yes. Freezing a row and freezing a column are independent — you can set both, and the combination is often called freezing a "pane."

Will freezing rows slow down my spreadsheet?

Freezing a reasonable number of rows has no noticeable effect. Freezing a very large number of rows in an already large spreadsheet can occasionally add a slight lag.
