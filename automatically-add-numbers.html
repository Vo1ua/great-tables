How to Automatically Add Numbers in Google Sheets

Manually re-typing a total every time your data changes gets old fast. The good news: Google Sheets can keep totals updated on its own, so a new sale, expense, or entry automatically rolls into the sum without you touching the formula again. Here's how to set that up, from the quickest built-in shortcut to a fully self-updating range.

The Fastest Way: AutoSum

If you just need a one-time total of a column, you don't need to type a formula at all.

  1. Click the cell right below your list of numbers.
  2. Look for the Σ (Sigma) icon in the toolbar.
  3. Click it and select SUM from the dropdown.
  4. Google Sheets highlights the range it thinks you want — press Enter to confirm.
AutoSum is a shortcut for typing the formula yourself, not a separate feature — it just guesses the range so you don't have to select it manually.

The SUM Formula (More Control)

Typing the formula directly gives you more control over exactly which cells are included:

=SUM(B2:B13)

This adds every value from B2 through B13. You can also combine individual cells with a range in the same formula:

=SUM(B2:B10, B15)

Making the Total Update Automatically as You Add Rows

A fixed range like B2:B13 won't include a new number you type into B14. To make the total truly automatic — updating no matter how many rows you add — use an open-ended range instead:

=SUM(B2:B)

Leaving off the ending row number tells Google Sheets to sum everything from B2 down to the last row of the sheet. Add a new number in B14, B50, or B500, and the total updates instantly — no editing the formula required.

Automatically Filling a Formula Down a Column

Sometimes "automatic" means applying the same calculation to every row, not just a single total. For example, if column D should always equal column B minus column C:

  1. Type the formula in the first row: =B2-C2
  2. Click the cell to select it, then find the small blue square in its bottom-right corner — the Fill Handle.
  3. Click and drag it down through the rows you want to fill.

Google Sheets automatically adjusts the cell references for each row, so row 3 becomes =B3-C3, row 4 becomes =B4-C4, and so on — this is called relative referencing.

Adding Numbers That Meet a Condition

Not every total should include everything. If you only want to add numbers that match a specific condition — like sales from one region, or expenses over $100 — use SUMIF instead:

=SUMIF(range, criterion, [sum_range])

For example, to total only the values in column B where column A says "East":

=SUMIF(A2:A100, "East", B2:B100)

Common Issues

Total Shows 0 or Doesn't Update

This usually means some cells in the range contain text rather than numbers — currency symbols like $ or £ are sometimes read as text. Double-check that every cell in the range holds a plain number.

#N/A or Unexpected Errors

If you're mixing SUM with other functions, make sure parentheses are balanced and ranges don't accidentally overlap with unrelated data below your table.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the total update if I change a number in the range?

Yes. As long as the changed cell falls within the range referenced in the formula, the total recalculates instantly.

Can I add only specific cells instead of a whole range?

Yes — list them individually, separated by commas: =SUM(A1, A3, A5).

What's the difference between SUM and AutoSum?

AutoSum is just the toolbar shortcut that writes a SUM formula for you automatically. Once inserted, it works exactly the same as a manually typed SUM formula.
