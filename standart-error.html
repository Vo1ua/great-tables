How to Calculate Standard Error in Google Sheets

Standard error (technically, the standard error of the mean) tells you how much the average of a sample is likely to differ from the true average of the whole population it came from. Unlike SUM or AVERAGE, Google Sheets doesn't have one single function for it — but the formula to build it yourself is short and easy to reuse once you have it.

The Formula

Standard error is calculated as the standard deviation divided by the square root of the sample size. In Google Sheets, that translates to:

=STDEV.S(range)/SQRT(COUNT(range))

Here's what each part is doing:

  • STDEV.S(range) — calculates the standard deviation of your sample
  • COUNT(range) — counts how many numeric values are in that same range
  • SQRT(...) — takes the square root of that count

Worked Example

Say you have 10 test scores in cells A2 through A11:

A
82
88
91
76
95

In an empty cell, enter:

=STDEV.S(A2:A11)/SQRT(COUNT(A2:A11))

This returns a single number — the standard error of the mean for that sample. A smaller result means the scores are clustered tightly around the average; a larger result means they're more spread out.

Interpreting the Result

  • Larger standard error: values in the dataset are more spread out around the mean
  • Smaller standard error: values are clustered closely around the mean
  • Larger sample size: tends to produce a smaller standard error, since more data usually gives a more reliable estimate
If you double the number of data points in a sample without changing how spread out they are, the standard error shrinks — this is the mathematical reason "more data" is generally considered more trustworthy in statistics.

Population vs. Sample Standard Deviation

The formula above uses STDEV.S, which assumes your data is a sample from a larger population — the most common case. If your data actually represents the entire population rather than a sample of it, use STDEV.P instead:

=STDEV.P(range)/SQRT(COUNT(range))

Common Issues

#DIV/0! Error

This happens when the range contains fewer than two numeric values, since standard deviation can't be calculated from a single data point. Check that your range actually includes enough numbers.

Result Looks Too Large or Too Small

Double-check that the range in COUNT matches the range in STDEV.S exactly. A mismatch — for example, including a header row in one but not the other — throws off the entire calculation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is there a single STANDARD_ERROR function in Google Sheets?

No. Standard error has to be built by combining STDEV.S, SQRT, and COUNT into one formula, since Google Sheets doesn't offer a single built-in function for it.

What does a larger standard error mean?

A larger standard error means the values in the dataset are more spread out around the mean. A smaller standard error means the data points are clustered closer to the average.

Does sample size affect standard error?

Yes. As sample size increases, standard error tends to decrease, since a larger sample generally gives a more reliable estimate of the true population mean.
