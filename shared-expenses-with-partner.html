How to Track Shared Expenses with a Partner in Google Sheets

When two people share bills, groceries, and rent, the hard part usually isn't recording the numbers — it's remembering who paid for what, and figuring out who owes whom at the end of the month. A simple Google Sheets setup can handle this automatically, so neither of you has to do mental math or dig through receipts to settle up.

Step 1: Set Up a Transaction Log

Create one tab called Transactions where every shared expense gets logged as it happens, with one row per expense:

DateDescriptionAmountPaid By
7/1Rent1200Partner A
7/3Groceries85Partner B
7/10Electric bill60Partner A

Keep it this simple at first — date, what it was, how much, and who actually paid. The formulas below do the rest of the work.

Step 2: Calculate Total Paid by Each Partner

On a separate Summary tab, use SUMIF to total how much each person has paid so far:

=SUMIF(Transactions!D:D, "Partner A", Transactions!C:C)

This adds up every amount in column C of the Transactions tab, but only for rows where column D says "Partner A." Repeat with "Partner B" in a separate cell.

Step 3: Calculate Each Partner's Fair Share

For a straightforward 50/50 split, each partner's fair share is simply half the total of all shared expenses:

=SUM(Transactions!C:C)/2

If you split everything evenly, both partners have the same "fair share" value — the formula above only needs to be written once and referenced by both.

Step 4: Calculate the Net Balance (Who Owes Whom)

This is the number that actually answers the question everyone wants to know. Subtract each partner's fair share from what they've actually paid:

=(Total Paid by Partner A) - (Fair Share)

  • Positive number: that partner overpaid and is owed money
  • Negative number: that partner underpaid and owes the difference

For example, if Partner A paid $1,260 total and the fair share was $1,197.50, the net balance is +$62.50 — meaning Partner B owes Partner A $62.50 to even things out.

Handling Splits That Aren't 50/50

If you split costs proportionally to income instead of evenly, add a Split % column to the Transactions tab (for example, 60% / 40%). Then calculate each partner's fair share as:

=SUMPRODUCT(Transactions!C:C, Transactions!E:E)

Where column E holds each partner's split percentage for that row. This multiplies every expense by the relevant percentage and adds the results together, so a mixed split (some expenses 50/50, others weighted differently) is handled automatically.

Common Issues

SUMIF Returns 0

Check that the name typed in the "Paid By" column exactly matches the name used in the SUMIF formula — including spelling and capitalization. A stray space at the end of a cell is a common, hard-to-spot cause.

Net Balance Doesn't Match What We Expect

Double-check that every shared expense is actually logged in the Transactions tab — a single missed entry, especially a large one like rent, will throw off the whole balance calculation.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I split an expense that isn't 50/50?

Add a Split % column and multiply the total by that percentage for each partner's share, instead of dividing evenly by two.

Can both partners edit the sheet at the same time?

Yes. Google Sheets supports real-time collaboration, so both partners can add transactions simultaneously and see each other's entries update live.

What does a positive or negative net balance mean?

A positive balance means that partner has paid more than their share and is owed money. A negative balance means they owe the difference to the other partner.
